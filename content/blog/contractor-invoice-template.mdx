Every contractor has been there: you finish the job, send a rough invoice, and then wait. And wait. The problem usually isn't the customer — it's the invoice. A professional, complete invoice signals that you run a tight operation and expect to be paid on time.

This guide covers exactly what to include on a contractor invoice, how to set payment terms that work in your favor, and how to get it all done without spending an hour on paperwork after every job.

## What to Include on Every Contractor Invoice

A contractor invoice needs to convey authority and professionalism while giving the customer everything they need to pay you. Here are the required fields:

**Your business details**
- Company name, logo (if you have one)
- Business address
- Phone number and email
- License number (required in most jurisdictions)

**Client details**
- Customer name and company (if commercial)
- Job site address
- Billing address if different

**Invoice identifiers**
- Unique invoice number (e.g., INV-2026-047)
- Invoice date
- Due date
- Purchase order number if the client requires one

**Job description**
- Line items for each service or material
- Quantity and unit price per line item
- Subtotal per line
- Any discounts applied

**Totals**
- Subtotal
- Tax (HST/GST in Canada, sales tax where applicable)
- Total amount due

**Payment instructions**
- Accepted payment methods (e-transfer, cheque, credit card)
- Bank details or payment link if applicable
- Late payment policy

## Setting Payment Terms That Actually Get You Paid

The biggest mistake contractors make is writing "Net 30" and hoping for the best. Here's what actually works:

**Net 14 for residential, Net 30 for commercial.** Homeowners generally have the cash on hand — there's no reason to give them 30 days. Use shorter terms for residential jobs.

**Deposit upfront.** For jobs over $1,000, collect 30–50% before starting. This filters out slow payers before you've done any work.

**Spell out late fees.** "1.5% per month on overdue balances" sounds professional and gives you leverage. Most customers won't let it come to that, but it sets expectations.

**Offer e-transfer.** The easier you make it to pay, the faster you get paid. Cheques introduce a 3–5 day mailing delay. E-transfer is instant.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**No invoice number.** Without a unique number, you can't reference the invoice in follow-up emails. Start at INV-001 and increment.

**Vague descriptions.** "Electrical work" is not a line item. Break it down: panel upgrade, labour, materials, permit. This also protects you if there's a dispute.

**No due date.** "Payment upon receipt" means nothing. Put an explicit date.

**Missing your license number.** In most provinces and states, your license number is legally required on invoices and contracts for licensed trades.

**No late fee policy.** Without one, you have no leverage. Add it to your invoice template and every customer will see it.
